Cane Stabilizer

Designing a one-handed, self-stabilizing cane attachment for a user with hemiparesis

Introduction

This project was part of MIT's Assistive Technology (AT) design program, where student teams are paired with a real co-designer to build something that addresses a specific need in their life. Our co-designer was Susan Bibbins, a retired children's psychologist living with fibromyalgia and hemiparesis, which leave her with one reliably functional hand. Susan uses a cane daily.

Problem statement: Current cane stabilizers are too bulky, heavy, and obstructive. We set out to create an external attachment to keep Susan's cane upright when not in use.

Design requirements:

  • Doesn't impede the cane's functionality
  • Accessible for someone with one functional hand
  • Lightweight and self-stabilizing when deployed
  • Does not interfere with footing (not a trip hazard)
  • Able to withstand different weather conditions
  • Externally motorized

Our co-designer, Susan Bibbins

Concept Selection

Early work explored internal cane modifications, but there was no space to do anything inside the cane, so we moved to external designs. We evaluated concepts along two axes:

We landed on a spring-loaded leg linkage system actuated by a motor and string.

Current Design

The full system breaks down into six subsystems:

Motor Housing and Spool

The motor housing keeps the motor low-profile and firmly affixed to the cane. The motor and battery housings attach to the upper section of the cane with a ligature. The housings compress around the motor, battery, and switch so nothing moves. All enclosures are waterproofed so the cane can be used in any conditions.

The spool is the interface between motor and string. The motor spins the spool, which wraps and unwraps the string around it.

Ligature Connections & Leg Linkage Design

The ligature serves as a spring holder with a feature to connect the legs. The tightening mechanism was inspired by a clarinet reed ligature.

My focus on the team was the leg and linkage geometry. The legs are connected by two rings: one non-translating (with a manual tightening mechanism) and one translating (which converts vertical motion into leg extension). Hooks on both rings connect springs that drive the legs outward. Material was added to the bottom of each leg for grip.

The key constraint was making sure each leg makes flush contact with the ground, not just a point or edge that would rock. The linkage pivot point sits above the leg's centerpoint so that the geometry sweeps the foot flat at full extension. Getting this wrong by even a few millimeters meant the cane would sit on a corner of the leg rather than a flat face, making it unstable and wearing down the material.

String Mechanism and Motor Controller

A braided UHMWPE string runs along the cane using custom 3D-printed attachment points. One end fastens to the translating ring; the other anchors to the spool on the motor shaft. When the motor rotates in one direction, the spool winds the string, lifting the translating ring and retracting the legs. Rotating the opposite direction unwinds the string, allowing 3x38mm tension springs to extend the legs downward.

The initial prototype used an Arduino Nano to control a servo. The final design uses a motor, SPDT rocker switch, and 9V battery.

FEA Analysis for Material Choice

Materials considered (based on availability and feasibility):

  • 3D-printed: PLA, TPU
  • Plastics: Delrin, PVC
  • Non-plastics: Aluminum, Wood

FEA setup: Optimizing leg length, width, and thickness. Incrementally increasing axial, vertical, and horizontal loads until the leg reached yield strength.

Additional analysis:

  • Max weight: 0.32 kg (0.705 lbs) based on existing cane tip
  • Max force for motor: 18 N (max weight of stopper × 3 spring force)
  • Center of mass calculation to ensure stability

Further Work

Durability: Analyze the stabilizer's lifespan to determine how frequently parts need replacement. Parts should be easily replaceable.

Terrain adaptability: Improve the stabilizer to function reliably on unpaved, uneven, and slippery surfaces.

Open sourcing: Make documentation publicly available for anyone to build their own stabilizer using affordable, readily accessible materials and tools.
