OX Truck Vibration Analysis

Benchmarking whole-body vibration exposure on the OX4 truck against a Toyota Hilux across six test track routes

Skills

Data AnalysisVibration TestingTechnical ReportingISO Standards

Timeline

Jun – Aug 2025

3 months

Collaborators

OX Delivers Engineering Team

As part of my engineering internship at OX Delivers in Leamington, England, I conducted a whole-body vibration (WBV) analysis comparing the OX4 truck's suspension performance against a Toyota Hilux, a well-established benchmark in the light commercial vehicle segment. The goal was to quantify driver and passenger vibration exposure across OX's test track routes and assess compliance with UK health and safety regulations.

Background

Whole-body vibration is a regulated occupational health risk in the UK under the Control of Vibration at Work Regulations 2005. Two thresholds govern how long a driver can be exposed before action is required:

  • EAV (Exposure Action Value): the daily exposure level above which an employer must take steps to reduce risk
  • ELV (Exposure Limit Value): the maximum permissible daily exposure; this must not be exceeded

For context: the more time-to-EAV a route produces, the better the ride, since it means a driver could spend more hours on that surface before hitting the action threshold. Routes with short time-to-EAV values (under an hour) indicate aggressive vibration that requires scheduling interventions and exposure limits, particularly for older drivers, pregnant workers, or those with back conditions.

Test Routes

Data was collected across six distinct track surfaces at OX's test facility, each designed to simulate different real-world driving conditions:

  • City Route: general urban surface simulation
  • High Speed Bowl: 60 kph cruising with repeated 60–40 kph snubs
  • Twist Humps: repeated vertical impacts
  • Belgian Pavé: cobblestone surface, the harshest route
  • Hill Route (Loops 1 & 2): gradient and terrain variation
  • Berm Road: lateral and vertical combined inputs

Each route was driven in multiple configurations: OX4 unladen, OX4 at GVW (gross vehicle weight), Hilux 1UP (driver only), and Hilux at GVW. Measurements were taken at the driver's seat, passenger's seat, and steering wheel.

Results

The table below shows time-to-EAV and time-to-ELV for each route and configuration. Longer times indicate lower vibration severity.

Time-to-EAV and time-to-ELV across all six routes, OX4 vs Hilux, unladen and GVW

A few findings stood out:

  • Belgian Pavé was the most severe surface across all vehicles. EAV was reached in as little as 33 minutes on the Hilux GVW driver's seat, and under 90 minutes on the OX4 in most configurations. This route would require strict scheduling controls for regular use.
  • High Speed Bowl and City Route produced the mildest exposure levels, with time-to-EAV exceeding 6 hours on the OX4 unladen driver's seat.
  • The OX4 at GVW generally showed longer time-to-EAV than unladen on smoother routes, suggesting the added mass damps out higher-frequency inputs, though this reversed on rougher surfaces like Twist Humps.
  • The Hilux performed comparably to the OX4 on most routes, with the Belgian Pavé being the clearest differentiator between the two platforms.

OX test track facility, Hill Route section

Deliverable

I compiled the full analysis into an internal engineering report, including route-by-route EAV/ELV breakdowns, comparison charts between the OX4 and Hilux, and risk mitigation recommendations for routes where exposure limits could be reached within a standard working day. The report also outlined scheduling guidelines with recommended maximum daily durations per route, to keep driver exposure within legal limits during extended testing programs.
