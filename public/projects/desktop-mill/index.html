Smart Mill UROP

Assembling and instrumenting an intelligent CNC vertical milling machine for human-machine collaboration research

The MIT Learning Engineering and Practice Group (LEAP), led by Dr. John Liu, is building the next generation of human-machine collaboration tools for manufacturing. Their flagship project — the Smart Mill — pairs a human operator with an intelligent robotic system mounted on a CNC vertical mill, with feedback delivered through extended reality (XR). I joined as a UROP researcher in Spring 2025, working directly under Dr. Rolando Bautista Montesano.

The Problem

Most human-machine collaboration systems today are built for simple, repetitive tasks. They rely on pre-programmed motions and can't adapt to real-time changes or interact dynamically with a human operator. This makes them ineffective for high-mix, low-volume manufacturing — exactly the kind of flexible, skilled work that still requires human judgment. The Smart Mill aims to change that by combining sensing, cognitive modeling, and XR feedback into a system that learns from expert operators and helps train new ones.

My Role

My work focused on the physical bring-up of the machine and building the documentation infrastructure around it:

  • Assembled and tested the CNC desktop vertical milling machine, validating precision, repeatability, and mechanical function from the ground up
  • Co-authored instructional documentation for operating the mill, written to train future students and lab technicians building skills on the machine

Instructional Documentation

As we brought the mill online, we wrote step-by-step guides covering machine setup, probing, coordinate systems, and safe operation — drawing directly on what we encountered during assembly and testing. The goal was to lower the barrier for the next cohort of students using the machine, particularly those with no prior CNC experience. Writing it forced us to think carefully about where assumptions were hiding in our own understanding of the machine.

Co-authored instructional documentation for student training

Reflection

Most of my engineering coursework starts with a design problem and ends with a prototype. This UROP flipped that — I started with a physical machine and worked toward making it ready for research. The assembly phase taught me a lot about how precision is built up (and lost) incrementally, and the documentation work reinforced something I hadn't fully appreciated before: that a tool is only as useful as people's ability to use it confidently.
