Refrigerated Seed Dispenser

Designing an automated cold stratification system to help community gardeners grow native plants

Skills

User ResearchProduct DesignThermoelectric CoolingRapid PrototypingLaser Cutting

Timeline

Jan – May 2024

14 weeks

Collaborators

Austen Simpson

Dalila Valdes

Nader Jemel

In MIT's 2.00 Introductory Product Design course, our team was assigned the theme of "Gathering" and matched with volunteers from the Somerville Gardening Club and Somerville Growing Center, two organizations focused on promoting native plant cultivation in the community.

The Problem

The volunteers were already running community events and handing out seed packets, but they repeatedly encountered the same issue: many native Boston seeds require cold stratification before germination can occur. These seeds naturally experience a cold winter period in the ground, but without mimicking that process artificially, they simply won't sprout when planted in spring.

Most gardeners don't know this, and there was no easy way for the volunteers to both store seeds at the right temperature and explain the process to someone picking up a packet at a community event. Education and storage were two separate problems with no unified solution.

Site visit to the Somerville Growing Center

Existing seed storage — no temperature control

Design Process

We explored several concepts before converging on a refrigerated seed library: a dispenser that could live in a library, school, or community center, keep seeds cold for stratification, and surface educational content right at the point of pickup. After an initial round of user feedback, we settled on a hexagonal form with three small drawers on each of the six sides, giving us 18 compartments for different seed varieties.

Key design decisions:

  • Thermoelectric cooling with Peltier plates and aluminum tubing: simple, reliable, no compressor required
  • Polyurethane foam walls for insulation and structural rigidity, easy to machine for rapid prototyping
  • Lazy Susan rotating base so users can spin the dispenser to reach any drawer without moving around it
  • QR codes on seed packets linking to growing instructions, so education travels with the seed
  • Plant photos on drawer faces so users know what they're getting before they pick it up

Demo of the refrigerated seed dispenser

Fabrication

The team split fabrication responsibilities: I assembled the base, laser cut the exhaust panels for the cooling system, and spray painted the body for a clean finish. The full build was a compressed, collaborative push over the final weeks of the semester.

Final prototype at the product showcase

Reflection

The most valuable part of this project was how early and often we talked to our users. The hexagonal shape, the Lazy Susan, the QR codes, none of those came from the initial brief. They all came from going back to the Somerville gardeners after our first concept and asking what would actually make this useful at a community event. The design improved every time we did that.
