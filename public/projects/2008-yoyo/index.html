2.008 Yo-Yo

Designing and manufacturing 50 yo-yos using injection molding, thermoforming, and CNC machining

2.008 (Design and Manufacturing II) is MIT's production-scale manufacturing course. Rather than building one-off prototypes, each team designs a yo-yo and then manufactures 50 complete units using the same industrial processes found in real factories — injection molding, thermoforming, and CNC machining. The course is as much about process as it is about design.

This page documents the project as it unfolds. We're currently past the Design Review (Week 6) with molds machined and first injection molded parts in hand.

The Goal

Design a yo-yo that meets all class constraints, can be manufactured repeatably at quantity, and holds together via a press fit — no glue, no fasteners holding the halves together. The engineering challenge is less about the shape and more about controlling tolerances across a multi-step manufacturing process so that the press fit is consistent across all 50 units.

Design

Our yo-yo consists of two injection molded body halves, a thermoformed ring, and an insert-molded nut for the axle. Key design constraints we worked within:

  • At least 2 injection molded parts and 1 thermoformed part
  • Draft angles on all IM surfaces for clean ejection
  • Uniform wall thickness to prevent sink marks and warping
  • Press fit sized to account for shrinkage — modeled in Fusion 360 and validated against the manufacturing spreadsheet
  • All features machinable on a 3-axis CNC mill

Press Fit Engineering

Getting the press fit right required working backwards from the desired interference at room temperature, accounting for the shrinkage of the specific plastic we're using. We modeled shrinkage in Fusion 360 and scaled our mold CAD accordingly — parts come out of the mold slightly oversized, and shrink to their final target dimensions as they cool.

Manufacturing spreadsheet — shrinkage and press fit parameters

CNC Machining the Molds

Molds were programmed in Fusion 360 using 2.5D and 3D CAM toolpaths, then cut on the HAAS VF2ss VMC in the LMP machine shop. We used probing to set work offsets precisely before each run. Getting the CAM right — choosing the right tools, stepdowns, and finishing passes — was its own design challenge.

Injection Molding

With molds machined, we moved to the injection molding machines — a Boy and an Arburg. The process involved reaming ejector pin holes to final size, setting up MUD frames, dialing in shot size, and troubleshooting short shots and flash. First parts are out.

Up Next

The project is ongoing — still ahead of us are thermoforming the ring, measuring parts on the CMM to validate shrinkage predictions, peer reviews, and ultimately assembling and testing all 50 yo-yos before the final presentation in Week 15.
